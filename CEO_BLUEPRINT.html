4. The Problem We Solve

The Operational Ceiling

Creator agencies hit a growth ceiling that has nothing to do with demand for their services or the quality of their creators. It is a pure operations problem — and it appears consistently around 10–20 creators.

The ceiling manifests as: "We can't take on any more creators right now." Not because of lack of demand — because the ops would break.

The 5 Root Causes

1. Content Volume Without Infrastructure

A 15-creator agency needs 75–300 pieces of content coordinated per month across multiple platforms and brand partners. There is no system. It is all manual. It breaks as the roster grows.

2. Brand Deal Pipeline Is Unmanaged

Deals move through 5–7 stages (outreach, negotiation, contract, live, reporting, payment). Tracking this for 10+ creators in a spreadsheet is a full-time job done badly. Deals fall through. Payments are missed. Brands don't come back.

3. Reporting Is Manual, Inconsistent, and Late

Reporting to creators, to brands, and internally takes hours every week. It is done differently each time, often late, and erodes trust on all sides.

4. AI Tools Exist But No One Has Built the System

Agency owners know ChatGPT, Notion AI, and similar tools exist. They have tried them. They do not have 40 hours to architect a working system from scattered tools — and they shouldn't have to.

5. Hiring Doesn't Scale Below a Certain Revenue Threshold

A content operations hire costs £28,000–£45,000/year in salary alone, plus recruitment, oncosts, management time, and turnover risk. For an agency doing £20,000–£60,000/month in revenue, this hire is viable — but fragile. One resignation sets operations back by months.

The result of removing these five constraints: An agency that was stuck at 12 creators can scale to 25 or 40 — without a proportional increase in headcount or operational stress. That is the revenue impact our retainer needs to be measured against.

5. Our Solution & System Architecture

What We Build and Run

For each client, we build a bespoke AI content operations system and manage it ongoing. The system covers five interconnected components:

  1. Creator Content Engine — AI-generated content for each creator, personalised by niche, platform, and voice. Scripts, captions, hooks, repurposing suggestions. Output: 5–10 pieces per creator per week.
  2. Brand Deal Pipeline — structured deal tracking from outreach to payment, visible in real time. No deal is missed. No stage is forgotten.
  3. Content Queue — all generated content organised by creator, platform, and status. Clients review and approve directly in the portal.
  4. Automated Reporting — weekly email reports sent to the agency every Monday morning. Content output, deals in progress, network summary. Zero manual work.
  5. Client Portal Access — a live, professional dashboard. Always available. Revoked immediately if the retainer lapses.

What We Don't Do (And Why)

  • We don't post content on behalf of creators — this creates platform liability; it is a natural expansion path once trust is established
  • We don't guarantee brand deal revenue — we can't control brands; we can control the pipeline quality and tracking
  • We implement and optimise alongside the client's existing strategy — we are not replacing strategic direction, we are removing the operational friction that stops strategy from executing

The fastest path to first revenue is direct, personalised LinkedIn outreach to decision-makers at UGC creator agencies. No paid ads. No events. No waiting for inbound.

First 1–3 clients are the most important thing we will ever close. Once results exist:

Identify referral partners who serve the same clients: creator economy lawyers, talent agency consultants, creator analytics platforms (Modash, Social Blade), accountants who work with creator businesses. Offer 10% of first month's retainer for qualified introductions.

12. First Client Strategy (Critical)

This is the most important section in the document. Everything changes after the first paying client. Without proof, every sales conversation is harder than it needs to be. Getting client 1 is the only priority.

The Reality of Pre-Proof Sales

Right now, every prospect will silently think:

  • "Does this actually work?"
  • "Is this business established?"
  • "What happens if it doesn't deliver?"

We have strong answers to all three — we have a live system, a live network, and a live portal. But without a client success story, we are asking them to be first. That requires extra trust-building.

The Pilot Framing

For the first 1–2 clients, frame the engagement as a founding client programme rather than a standard retainer. This does two things: it reduces perceived risk and it creates a sense of exclusivity.

"We're onboarding our first three agency partners at a founding rate — £1,500/month instead of £2,000, locked in for 6 months. In return, we ask for a case study and a testimonial if you're happy with the results. We're selective about who we take on at this stage."

Overdeliver Heavily on the First Client

The first client is not just a revenue event. It is a proof event. Treat it accordingly:

  • Generate more content than contracted
  • Check in more frequently than required
  • Flag anything in their pipeline proactively — don't wait to be asked
  • At 30 days, send a written summary of everything delivered with metrics
  • At 60 days, start the case study conversation

The First Client Changes Everything

After the first paying client:

  • Sales conversations open with proof instead of concept
  • Objections shift from "does this work?" to "how much?" — a tractable problem
  • LinkedIn content becomes operator reporting instead of positioning — more credible and more effective
  • Closing the second client takes roughly half the effort of closing the first

15. Objection Handling

Every prospect will raise one or more of these objections. Have a clean, confident answer ready.

Objection 1: "Why wouldn't I just hire someone?"

Answer: "A content coordinator costs £24,000–£40,000 a year before you factor in recruitment, oncosts, and management time. They get sick, leave, and need constant direction. With us, you pay less, get a complete system with a live portal and automated reporting, and nothing breaks when someone resigns. We're also available month to month — you're not locked into an employment contract."

Objection 2: "How is this different from just using ChatGPT?"

Answer: "ChatGPT is a tool. We're a system that runs itself — content generation tuned for each of your creators, a pipeline that tracks every deal, reports that go out every Monday without you touching anything, and a portal your team can check any time. Using ChatGPT yourself is like owning a hammer versus hiring a construction company. We build and manage the whole thing."

Objection 3: "What results can we actually expect?"

Answer: "The direct outputs are: 5–10 content pieces per creator per week, every brand deal tracked from outreach to payment, and a weekly report on all of it sent automatically. The business outcome those unlock is: you can take on more creators without adding headcount, and you have visibility on every deal without chasing anyone. We can show you the live system we use for our own network before you commit to anything."

Objection 4: "Do you have case studies or proof this works?"

Answer (pre-first client): "We don't have a client case study yet — we're onboarding our first agency partners now. What we do have is a live system we've built and run for our own creator network, which I can walk you through right now. You'd be seeing real content, a real pipeline, real reports — not a concept. We're also offering a founding client rate to the first three agencies we take on."

Objection 5: "What happens if we want to cancel?"

Answer: "30 days' notice, that's it. We don't lock you in. The system itself remains ours — that's how we keep the price this low — but you're never trapped. Most clients stay because the output is consistent and the value is clear, not because of a contract."

Objection 6: "£2,000/month feels expensive"

Answer: "Compare it to what it replaces: a part-time hire costs the same or more, delivers less, and comes with employment risk. The question is whether the output justifies it — and the best way to answer that is to see the live system and decide if you'd use it. Want to spend 20 minutes doing that before making any decision?"

18. Client Success & Retention

The Retention Problem to Solve

Our model depends on long LTV and low churn. The risk is clients treating CAIG as a "nice-to-have ops layer" rather than a revenue-critical system. The antidote is making our output visibly tied to business outcomes every single month.

Important: Clients will not think "content ops issue" when things go wrong. They will think "this isn't working." We must pre-empt that by consistently surfacing wins — not just output.

What "Visible Wins" Looks Like

  • "We generated X pieces of content this week — here's what's ready for review"
  • "This brand deal moved from Negotiation to Contract — here's the timeline"
  • "Your Monday report went out to all 3 creators automatically — here's a copy"
  • "You now have 14 content pieces queued across 3 creators — enough for 2 weeks of posting"

Wins should be surfaced in the portal, in the automated reports, and proactively in check-in messages. Never wait to be asked.

Churn Warning Signs

  • Client hasn't logged into the portal in 3+ weeks
  • Client is slow to respond to check-in messages
  • Client questions pricing without prompting
  • Client mentions "reviewing costs" or "reassessing tools"

Response: Proactively reach out within 48 hours. Offer a brief call. Present a written summary of recent output. If necessary, offer a temporary 20% discount rather than losing the retainer — recovering a client costs 10x more than retaining one.

Retention Mechanics

  • Monthly check-in call (Growth and Agency tiers): 20 minutes, show output, plan next month
  • Quarterly system review: Identify new capabilities to add, explore expansion to additional creators
  • Case study at 6 months: Clients who agree to a case study are significantly less likely to churn — they are invested in the relationship and the narrative

20. Legal, IP & Contractual Structure

Company Structure

Operate as a UK Limited Company (Ltd). Incorporate via Companies House (£50, same-day online). Provides limited liability, VAT registration capability, and professional credibility with clients.

Intellectual Property — The Non-Negotiable

We own the system. Permanently. Without exception. This must be in every contract.

  • The AI system, workflows, prompts, portal, and infrastructure built for the client remain CAIG's intellectual property
  • The client is purchasing access to a managed service — not a deliverable, not a product, not a licence
  • Access is contingent on active retainer payments
  • Termination: 30-day notice (client-initiated) or immediate (non-payment or material breach)

Master Services Agreement (MSA)

Required before any client signs. Have it drafted by a UK commercial solicitor (cost: £500–£1,500). Must cover:

  • Scope of service and deliverables
  • IP ownership (as above — in plain language, not buried in boilerplate)
  • Payment terms (setup fee upfront; monthly retainer by standing order)
  • Termination conditions and notice period
  • Confidentiality obligations on both sides
  • Data processing and GDPR compliance
  • Limitation of liability

GDPR Requirements

  • Privacy Policy on website (legally required — currently linked in footer)
  • Terms of Service / Client Agreement on website
  • Basic Record of Processing Activities (ROPA) — document what data we process and why
  • Ensure Supabase data is processed in EU or UK region
